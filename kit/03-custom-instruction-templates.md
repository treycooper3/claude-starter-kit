# Custom Instruction Templates — The Boardroom

> Custom instructions are the difference between Claude giving you generic MBA answers and Claude operating like a team member who knows your business, your voice, and your standards. Pick a template, fill in the blanks, and watch the difference immediately.

---

## How to Use This File

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Pick the template closest to your business |
| 2 | Copy the entire code block |
| 3 | Replace everything in `[BRACKETS]` with your actual info |
| 4 | Paste into **Claude.ai** (Project custom instructions) or save as `CLAUDE.md` for **Claude Code** |
| 5 | Delete sections that don't apply — shorter is better |

Every template here works for both Claude.ai and Claude Code. The format is identical. The only difference is where you put it:

- **Claude.ai** — Paste into a Project's custom instructions field
- **Claude Code** — Save as `CLAUDE.md` in your project root

Do not leave placeholder brackets in your final version. If a section doesn't apply, remove it entirely. Claude performs better with 20 lines of specific instructions than 60 lines of half-filled boilerplate.

## Architecture: WAT Framework
When building multi-step AI systems, follow the WAT pattern:
- **Workflows** — Markdown instructions defining what to do (the SOPs)
- **Agents** — AI handles reasoning and orchestration (the decision-maker)
- **Tools** — Deterministic scripts handle execution (the doers)
This separation keeps AI focused on what it's good at (reasoning) while code handles what it's good at (reliable execution).

## Combining Templates

Most businesses don't fit neatly into one category. If you're a contractor who also invests in real estate, or a developer who trades stocks on the side, combine sections from multiple templates.

How to do it:

1. Start with the template closest to your primary business
2. Pull in specific sections from other templates that apply
3. Keep total length reasonable — aim for 40-60 lines max in your final version
4. Group related instructions together under clear headers
5. Remove any duplicate or conflicting rules

For example, a general contractor who flips houses might combine Template 5 (Contracting) with the "Deal Criteria" and "Analysis Preferences" sections from Template 3 (Real Estate). A developer who runs an agency might merge Template 7 (Developer) with the "Client Types" and "Content Standards" sections from Template 2 (Marketing).

The goal is one unified document that covers how you operate, not a stack of disconnected templates.

## Quick Customization Checklist

Before you call your custom instructions done, verify these five things:

- [ ] **No brackets left** — every `[PLACEHOLDER]` has been replaced with real information
- [ ] **No empty sections** — if a section doesn't apply, it's been deleted entirely
- [ ] **Voice is defined** — Claude knows how you communicate, not just what you do
- [ ] **Rules are specific** — "always include pricing in proposals" beats "be professional"
- [ ] **It's under 60 lines** — tight and specific wins over long and vague every time
